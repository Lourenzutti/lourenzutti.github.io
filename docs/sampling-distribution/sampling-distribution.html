Sampling Distribution

In 2020, there was a presidential election in the United States. Every election year, we see many surveys trying to draw a picture of how people will vote. But how do they do these surveys? Do these polls check how all the people are going to vote? According to this table from US Census Bureau there are approximately 233.5 million voting-age citizens. It would certainly require a lot of resources to ask all 233.5 million voting-age American citizens (in fact, this is what the election period is for, right? and we all know how complicated and expensive is organizing the election).

Map by Free Vector Maps: http://freevectormaps.com

To make things more complicated, the political scenario changes quite fast. Therefore, time is also an issue. Even if you had the resources to ask everyone, many political events that swing voters’ opinions could happen during your survey, and by the time you are finished, your results are already outdated.

So, how can we learn (at least approximately) how voters are going to vote without asking every voter?

A natural answer for this question is: let us ask just a small portion of the voters how they are going to vote. This approach does provide us with a solution, but also many problems:

  1. How do we choose the voters to answer the survey?

  2. How many voters should answer the survey?

  3. How do we infer the knowledge obtained from the Sample to the entire population of voters?

  4. Different group of voters will have different proportion of votes.

In this chapter, we are going to focus on points 3 and 4. We will discuss points 1 and 2 in more detail later. Let’s formalize some intuitive concepts.

Population and parameters

Target Population

The group of all items/people that we are interested in.

In general, we are interested in one or more unknown summary quantities that describe the entire population (as opposed to specific individuals in the population), and they are called parameters.

Parameter

A summary quantity of the population that is (usually) unknown.

Parameters are unknown, we are trying to learn them because they tell us information about the population. Let’s see an example:

In the election example above, the group of all American voters is our population. Note that the population is not the group of all Americans. It is the group of all American voters. We are interested in the proportion of voters that will vote for each candidate, and the proportion of voters that will abstain from voting. In this case, we have more than one parameter of interest.

Caution

A common mistake many students make is to mix up a variable of interest with a parameter of interest. For example, one might be interested in the average dolphin weight, which is a parameter; the average weight is a quantity of the population, not of a single dolphin. However, the dolphin weight is what is being measured, i.e., the variable of interest.

In general, the population distribution is unknown because we cannot measure the variable of interest over the entire population. In these cases, we draw a sample from the population to learn about the population distribution.

Sample

We are going to call sample any subset (sub-group of elements) of the population. What we hope for is that this subset would represent the population well, but this is not always the case. Even if the population is poorly represented by a sub-group of elements, such sub-group is still called a sample of the population.

Sample

A subset of the population.

It is like when you are cooking. Imagine you’re making a soup and want to know if the soup has enough salt. Then, we usually just taste a spoon of the soup (a sample). If that portion that you tasted lacks salt, you conclude that the whole soup lacks salt. This process is the so-called inference; you are extrapolating results from a sample to the entire population. Now imagine you’re cooking less waterly food, say, french fries. Then, you take one piece to see if you have put enough salt. But, just by chance, you ended with a piece that got too much salt on top of it, and you conclude wrongly that the whole basket of french fries is salty. In this case, you got a sample that doesn’t represent the population well. But again, it’s still a sample.

There are many different strategies to draw a sample. One of the most common strategy is called random sampling without replacement. To take a random sampling without replacement of size n elements from a population you must:

  1. List all the elements of the population.

  2. Select one element at random from the population (all elements must have the same probability of being selected).

  3. Save the selected element and remove it from the population.

  4. Repeat Steps 2. and 3. n times.

Although random sampling without replacement is one of the simplest ways to draw a sample, it is still quite effective. However, in some cases, it is not the best strategy you could use. But let’s not worry about this now.

Now that you know the concept of sample, we need to revisit the concept of population. As it was mentioned, a sample is a subset of the population. But frequently the population that the sample is drawn from is not the same population we are interested in. Let us see an example.

In times of Covid-19, the Canadian government started providing some aid to Canadian workers that lost their jobs. The provincial government of British Columbia (BC) is studying if it should also provide some aid to university students. To make the decision, BC’s government wants to know the average income of university students in the province. There are many universities in BC, and sampling students from all universities can be costly and time-consuming. The government decides to sample students only from the University of British Columbia (UBC). By doing this, the sample that the government will be taking, comes from the population of all UBC students, which is a more restricted group than the group of all university students in BC.

Example 2.1 shows us that we need to improve our definition of population and make a distinction between the two populations.

Sampled Population

The group of all items/people which we are taking a sample from.

Ideally, the Sampled Population will coincide with the Target Population. If not, be aware that the sample results can only be extrapolated (by means of statistical inference) to the Sampled Population. To extrapolate from the Sampled Population to the Target Population, you need a justification beyond statistical inference – which could be valid but could be misleading and wrong. Careful!

It is also worth mentioning that in some cases the target population and sample population differ for reasons other than convenience. This is very common in medical research (see the exercise below).

Say that a Canadian pharmaceutical company created a drug for a new disease. But now, they need to test if the drug works on human beings. The company will administer the drug and a placebo on some people infected by the disease who are located Canada and compare the results to see if the drug works.

  1. What is the target population?


  2. What is the sampled population?


  3. What should the company do to match the sampled population to the target population? Is this reasonable?

Solution.

What is the target population?
The target population would be all the people in the world.
What is the sampled population?
People that are already sick with the disease in Canada.
What should the company do to match the sampled population to the target population? Is this reasonable?
The company would need to be able to infect anyone in the world (not only Canada), so it could administer the drug. This is clearly not reasonable. We cannot allow a company to go out there infecting people with a disease to see if a treatment works! In this case, ethical issues prevent us from matching the sample population with the target population.

Sample Distribution

The sample distribution is the distribution of the variable of interest over a given sample.

Let’s return to the STAT 100 grades example. But this time, we are going to take a random sample of 15 students to estimate the median grade.

Based on the Example 2.2, answer true or false:

  1. Each time we take a random sample we will have a different sample distribution.

  2. Each time we take a random sample we will have a different population distribution.

Solution.

Each time we take a random sample we will have a different sample distribution.
True!! Note that the sample distribution is the distribution of the sample you obtained! Since we sample at random, the sample distribution will change every time.
Each time we take a random sample we will have a different population distribution.
False!! Note that the sample is random, but the population is not. The population is fixed, with all the same individuals. Therefore, the population distribution is fixed.

Great! We now know some of the fundamental concepts we need. Let’s review the big picture.

Revisiting the Big Picture

  1. We are interested in a specific quantity (parameter) of a given population of objects/people. For example, the average income of all UBC students.

  2. Due to limited resources we cannot survey the whole population to obtain the exact value of the parameter;

  3. We then take a sample at random from the population.

  4. Next, we use the sample to learn about the parameter of the population. For example, if we are interested in the average income of a given population, we calculate the average income in the sample. The function/formula you are using is called estimator (e.g., sample mean, sample variance, sample quantile) and the value you obtain in a given sample is called estimate of the parameter.

Great, we are done! But wait for a second; the sample is random. The fact that we are using Chen, Mary, and Amy’s average income is just due chance. It could have been Ronald, Mary, and Lien, or Jean, Wang, and Adebola, or any other three UBC students. Different samples would give different results.

You might even end up with a sample that is not representative of the population. For example, imagine you select three unemployed students. The average income in your sample would be smaller than the average income of all UBC’s students, since not all UBC’s students are unemployed.

How can we rely on an approach if every time we take a sample, we get a different estimate?

Admittedly, since the estimate is a random value, the chances are that we will not hit the mark and get an estimate that exactly matches the value of the parameter of interest. However, we don’t need to. We just need a method that gives us estimates that are “close enough most of the time”.

Sampling distribution

When trying to learn about an unknown parameter, taking a sample and getting an estimate is a very reasonable approach. However, different samples will provide different estimates bringing uncertainty to the results. Think about, without knowing the population, how can you know that the sample you got represents the population well and will yield a good estimate for the parameter?

To better understand the variation of the estimates caused by sampling variation, we could obtain the sample mean for each one of the possible samples of a given size. For example, considering all possible samples of 3 UBC students, how many samples provide a sample average income between C$00.00 and C$50.00, between C$50.00 and C$100.00, and so on? In other words, we could consider the sample mean of all possible samples and study its distribution.

True or False: Each time we take a random sample we will have a different sampling distribution.
False!!! This can be confusing. But note that the sampling distribution is using the table of all possible samples. We are not concerned about the result in one sample only, but in all of them.

As you can see, the sampling distribution tells us a lot about our statistic. We could, for example, calculate the probability that our statistic would be to a certain range around the parameter. But, how is this useful if we need to have access to all possible samples? In fact, if we had access to all possible samples, we would be better off just checking out the entire population. In our very small example above, we have 20 elements in the population but 1140 possible samples of size 3. Luckily for us, we do not need to have access to all possible samples to know the sampling distribution. We don’t even need to know much about the population itself. This is so awesome that I will say it again: we are able to approximate the sampling distribution of a statistic even without having access to the population! All we need is a sample! The same sample that you use to calculate the statistic will also give you information on the uncertainty of your estimate.

Which of the following elements will affect the sampling distribution (keeping everything else constant)? Select all that apply:






Solution.

Population distribution
True! The population distribution will certainly affect the sampling distribution. For example, if you have more variability in the population, you will most probably have more variability in the estimates as well.
Sample distribution
False! The sample distribution changes according to the sample obtained, and consequently the estimate. But the sampling distribution is not about one sample, it is about all possible samples.
Sample size
True! As we increase the sample size, the estimator tends to vary less from sample to sample, which would make its sampling distribution narrower.
Estimator
True! The sampling distribution is the distribution of a given estimator. If you change the estimator, you are also changing the sampling distribution. For example, imagine that you want to estimate the weight of the lightest fish in the aquarium. So, instead of the sample mean, you would calculate the sample minimum. The values in the table shown in Example 4.1 would be completely different, and consequently, so would be the sampling distribution.
